What is Linear Transformation?

In mathematics, a linear transformation is at the core of linear algebra. A number plane has x and y axes. These two axes have corresponding "unit vectors". These are [1,0] and [0,1]. A vector can be thought of as an arrow. The tail of this arrow sits at the origin and the tip is defined by a pair of numbers. These numbers, like the one I mentioned earlier, are often called "matrix" or "matrices" in the plural. The first number in our matrix tells us where the tip of this vector is in the horizontal direction and the second number tells us where the tip of this vector is in the vertical direction. In the video below, we apply the linear transformation that takes the unit vector x = [1,0] (in red) to a new vector [1,1] and the unit vector y = [0,1] (in green) to a new vector [-1,0]. This transformation is applied to the entire number plane as you can see.

What is Vector Field Flow?

Imagine a 2D plane where at each point you have a defined vector. This means at every x and y value, you can compute a direction and a magnitude. This idea gives rise to a vector field. It turns out that such a tool is very useful in many areas of mathematics and physics. You can analyse things like the population dynamics of predators and pray to the fluid dynamics of liquids or gases. The rule that defines a given vector field can also be time-dependent. This means we have an extra parameter t to toy with. In fluid dynamics, we can apply physical laws, such as the continuity equation or the Navier-Stokes equation to make sure energy and mass are conserved.



What is the Bohr Model of the Atom?

Before the formulation of Quantum Mechanics, scientists had created many different models of the atom. A very successful model was presented by Niels Bohr and Ernest Rutherford in the early 20th century. Their model was able to describe the Rydberg formula and the emission spectrum of atoms. When atoms are excited, they produce characteristic wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation. The Bohr model described these as levels of electron excitations. When an excited electron spontaneously jumps down to a lower level, the difference in energy is released as a photon with the same energy. This model is very simple but it laid the groundwork for a more accurate model to be formulated later thanks to Quantum Mechanics. The model shows a positively charged nucleus is surrounded by negatively charged orbiting electrons. What is the Momentum Conservation Law?

Objects in motion have a quantitative property called "momentum". Momentum is defined as the velocity of the object times it's mass. And in a closed system, this property is conserved or kept constant from moment to moment. The video below shows two colliding blocks each having its own velocities. The formula at the end shows that the momentum of each object combined before the collision is equal to the momentum of each object combined after the collision. This is called the conservation of momentum and is a fundamental law of nature. From tiny atoms to rotating black holes many billions of times the mass of our sun all obey this law!
